⚠️ Did You Know?

Phishing attacks account for 90% of all data breaches. A successful phishing attack occurs every 39 seconds, costing organizations an average of $4.45 million per breach (IBM, 2023).

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyber attackA malicious attempt to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems that uses disguised email, websites, or messages as a weapon. The goal is to trick you into revealing sensitive information such as:

Passwords Credit Card Numbers Bank Account Details Social Security Numbers Personal Information

How Phishing Works

  1. The Bait: Attackers create a convincing message that appears to come from a trusted source
  2. The Hook: They create urgency or fear to pressure you into acting quickly
  3. The Catch: You click a malicious link or download an infected attachment
  4. The Damage: Your credentials are stolen, malware is installed, or data is encrypted

Remember: Legitimate organizations will NEVER ask for your password or sensitive information via email.

Recognizing Phishing Emails

🚩 Red Flags to Watch For

  • Urgent or threatening language - "Your account will be suspended!"
  • Generic greetings - "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Spelling and grammar errors - Professional organizations proofread carefully
  • Suspicious sender addresses - Check for misspelled domains
  • Unexpected attachments - Especially .exe, .zip, or .docm files
  • Requests for sensitive information - No legitimate company asks for passwords via email
  • Too-good-to-be-true offers - "You've won $1,000,000!"
  • Mismatched URLs - Hover over links to see the real destination

Real-World Example: Fake Bank Email

From: security@amaz0n-security.com
To: you@email.com
Subject: URGENT: Your Account Has Been Compromised

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your account will be SUSPENDED within 24 hours if you do not verify your information immediately.

Click here to verify: https://www.amazon.com/verify-account

Failure to respond will result in permanent account closure.

Sincerely,
Amazon Security Team

🚨 RED FLAGS IDENTIFIED:
• Sender domain is "amaz0n-security.com" (zero instead of 'o') — NOT amazon.com
• Creates false urgency with threats
• Generic greeting "Dear Valued Customer"
• Requests immediate action without verification
• Hovering over the link reveals a different URL

Quiz: Spot the Phishing Email

1. You receive an email from "paypal-security.net" asking you to verify your account. What should you do?
Click the link and enter your credentials to secure your account
Check the sender domain carefully and log in directly through paypal.com
Reply to the email asking if it's legitimate
Forward the email to your friends to warn them
Correct! Always verify the sender domain. "paypal-security.net" is NOT "paypal.com". Never click links in suspicious emails—go directly to the official website by typing the URL yourself.
2. An email says "Your package delivery failed. Click here to reschedule." The sender is "ups-delivery@ups-shipping.info". Is this legitimate?
Yes, it mentions UPS and shipping
No, the domain "ups-shipping.info" is suspicious
Yes, if you are expecting a package
Maybe, I should click to check
Correct! The domain "ups-shipping.info" is not the official UPS domain (ups.com). Attackers often register lookalike domains to trick users. Always verify through official channels.
3. Which of these is a common phishing tactic?
Sending a thank-you note for your recent purchase
Creating urgency with threats of account suspension
Providing a detailed receipt for a transaction
Sending a newsletter with company updates
Correct! Phishers frequently use urgency and fear ("Account will be suspended!", "Immediate action required!") to pressure victims into acting without thinking. Take time to verify before acting.

Recognizing Fake Websites

How Attackers Create Fake Sites

Phishing websites are designed to look identical to legitimate sites. They often use:

  • Homograph attacks: Using similar-looking characters (e.g., "rn" looks like "m")
  • Subdomain tricks: "secure-bankofamerica.com.evil-site.com"
  • HTTPS deception: Having a padlock icon doesn't mean the site is legitimate
  • Domain typos: "gooogle.com", "faceb00k.com"

URL Comparison Exercise

URL Status Why?
https://www.chase.com ✓ LEGITIMATE Correct domain, proper spelling, HTTPS
https://www.chase-secure-login.com ✗ FAKE Wrong domain - not chase.com
https://www.chase.com.verify-account.net ✗ FAKE Subdomain trick - actual domain is verify-account.net
https://www.chаse.com ✗ FAKE Uses Cyrillic 'а' instead of Latin 'a' (homograph attack)

🔍 How to Verify a Website

  1. Check the URL carefully - Look for misspellings and extra words
  2. Look for the padlock - But remember: HTTPS ≠ legitimate
  3. Examine the content - Poor grammar, low-quality images, broken links
  4. Check contact information - Fake sites often lack real contact details
  5. Verify the certificate - Click the padlock to see who issued it
  6. When in doubt, type the URL yourself - Never click links in suspicious emails

Social Engineering Tactics

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It's not just technical—it's human hacking.

Common Social Engineering Techniques

🎭 Pretexting

Creating a fabricated scenario to gain access to information. Example: An attacker calls pretending to be from IT support, claiming they need your password to fix a "critical server issue."

"Hi, this is Mike from IT. We're experiencing a critical database error and need to verify your credentials to restore access. Can you provide your current password?"

😰 Baiting

Offering something enticing to trap the victim. Example: Leaving infected USB drives in parking lots labeled "Confidential - Salary Information 2024." Curiosity leads people to plug them in.

👤 Impersonation

Posing as a trusted individual or authority figure. Example: An email appearing to be from your CEO requesting an urgent wire transfer.

"Hi [Your Name], I'm in a meeting and can't access my corporate card. I need you to purchase 5 $500 gift cards immediately for a client. Send me the codes ASAP. - CEO"

🎁 Quid Pro Quo

Offering a service or benefit in exchange for information. Example: "I'll help you fix your computer if you give me remote access."

😢 Emotional Manipulation

Exploiting emotions like fear, greed, curiosity, or sympathy:

  • Fear: "Your account will be deleted!"
  • Greed: "You've won the lottery!"
  • Curiosity: "You won't believe what someone said about you..."
  • Sympathy: Fake charity requests after disasters
  • Authority: "This is the IRS. You owe back taxes."

Defense Strategy: When someone creates urgency or uses emotional pressure, that's your cue to STOP. Legitimate requests allow time for verification. Always verify through a separate, trusted channel.

Best Practices & Final Quiz

Essential Security Practices

✅ Do's

  • Verify sender identities - Check email addresses carefully
  • Hover before clicking - Preview the actual URL destination
  • Use multi-factor authentication (MFA) - Adds a critical security layer
  • Keep software updated - Patch vulnerabilities promptly
  • Use strong, unique passwords - Consider a password manager
  • Report suspicious emails - Use your organization's reporting tools
  • Verify unusual requests - Call the person directly using a known number
  • Check for HTTPS - But remember it's not a guarantee of legitimacy

❌ Don'ts

  • Don't click links in unsolicited emails
  • Don't download unexpected attachments
  • Don't provide personal information via email
  • Don't use public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions
  • Don't ignore security warnings from your browser
  • Don't assume HTTPS means a site is safe
  • Don't reuse passwords across multiple accounts
  • Don't rush when handling financial or sensitive requests

Final Knowledge Check

4. You receive a text message: "Your Amazon order #12345 has been shipped. Track here: [link]" You didn't order anything. What do you do?
Click the link to see what was ordered
Ignore it and check your Amazon account directly through the official app
Reply STOP to unsubscribe
Forward it to friends as a warning
Correct! Never click links in unexpected messages. Go directly to your account through the official app or website. Replying to spam can confirm your number is active.
5. A website has a padlock icon (🔒) and uses HTTPS. This means:
The website is definitely legitimate and safe
The connection is encrypted, but the site could still be fake
The website is government-approved
No phishing can occur on this site
Correct! HTTPS only means the connection is encrypted. Attackers can obtain SSL certificates for fake domains. Always verify the actual domain name, not just the padlock.
6. Your "CEO" emails you urgently requesting gift cards. Best response:
Buy the gift cards immediately - it's the CEO!
Reply to the email asking for confirmation
Call the CEO's known office number to verify the request
Forward the request to your manager
Correct! CEO fraud (BEC attacks) cost organizations $2.7 billion in 2022. Always verify unusual financial requests through a separate, trusted communication channel - never reply to the suspicious email itself.
